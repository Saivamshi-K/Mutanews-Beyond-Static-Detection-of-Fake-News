# Mutanews-Beyond-Static-Detection-of-Fake-News
The rapid spread of misinformation across digital platforms has become a critical challenge, undermining information reliability and eroding public trust. Social media enables content to reach vast audiences within minutes, making it increasingly difficult to control the dissemination of misleading or false narratives. While significant progress has been made in fake news detection, most existing approaches frame the problem as a static classification task, labeling content as either real or fake. This perspective overlooks a key characteristic of misinformation—its ability to evolve as it propagates, becoming more persuasive and harder to detect over time.

To address this limitation, this paper introduces MutaNews, a framework that models misinformation as a dynamic and continuously mutating phenomenon. The proposed system integrates a fine-tuned transformer-based classifier to determine the authenticity of news content with a generative language model that simulates realistic variations of verified information. These variations are created through controlled semantic transformations, including entity substitution, temporal modification, and causal reframing, allowing the system to mimic how misinformation naturally changes during real-world circulation.

To analyze the effect of these mutations, semantic similarity techniques are employed to measure the degree of deviation between original and modified content, referred to as mutation strength. In addition, a composite scoring mechanism is proposed to estimate the potential of a piece of information to spread widely. This scoring approach combines multiple factors, such as semantic change, contextual influence, and informational novelty, providing a more comprehensive assessment of how likely a mutated narrative is to gain traction across networks.

Experimental evaluation demonstrates that even moderate semantic alterations can significantly reduce the effectiveness of traditional detection models while simultaneously increasing the likelihood of content propagation. These findings highlight the limitations of static detection systems and emphasize the need for approaches that account for the evolving nature of misinformation. The proposed framework offers a step toward proactive detection by anticipating how false information may transform, thereby contributing to the development of more resilient and adaptive misinformation mitigation strategies.
